Research question

How does global AI investment shape the rate and pattern of AI adoption across industries and business functions — and what does that mean for labor?

We examine how global AI investment shapes the rate and pattern of AI adoption across industries and business functions. We build the argument in four stages: first a measure of national capacity (investment intensity, scaled by GDP); then concentration of global flows (unscaled levels, by industry and function); then a forecast of both aggregate growth and its allocation across industries; finally the labor consequences that follow from that trajectory.

STAGE 01 · CAPACITY
AI investment intensity as a proxy for national diffusion capacity
We construct AI investment intensity as private AI investment by country scaled by real GDP. Scaling by GDP lets us compare economies of very different sizes on the same footing — it’s a proxy for how much of a country’s productive capacity is being committed to AI, and therefore for the institutional ability to absorb and diffuse the technology.

Capacity — key findings The US expanded intensity 11.3× (0.110% → 1.242% of GDP, 2019–2025), concentrating global diffusion capacity. Singapore sustains the highest average intensity in the panel, peaking at 0.49% of GDP in 2024 as APAC’s AI hub despite being a small economy. China’s 2025 intensity (0.064%) sits well below its 2021 peak (0.106%); US export controls on advanced semiconductors (Stanford AI Index 2025) and domestic macro pressure are visible in the trend. Germany and France remain below 0.16% of GDP — well behind the US and Singapore — consistent with the diffusion-capacity gap EU AI policy references.
STAGE 02 · CONCENTRATION
Where aggregate investment is concentrating, in levels
Now we switch to unscaled global levels to identify where investment growth is actually going. Intensity told us which countries can diffuse; levels tell us which industries and functions are absorbing the capital. Because we’re interested in the global distribution of flows, GDP-scaling would obscure the thing we want to see. Throughout this stage we use private AI investment specifically — the same measure used to fit the forecast in Stage 3 — rather than total corporate activity (which would also include M&A and public offerings).

Concentration — key findings Adoption is highest in information-intensive sectors: Tech (70%→92%), Professional Services (50%→82%), and Financial Services (55%→80%) (McKinsey / Stanford AI Index 2025). These industries produce outputs that are already digital — reports, code, financial analysis — making AI integration easier than in sectors with large physical-operations components. Physical-heavy sectors (Agriculture 15%→42%, Real Estate 18%→46%) are still growing but from a lower base. On functions, IT & Software Engineering jumped from 17% to 43% in four years (McKinsey Global Survey) — the clearest signal that capital is flowing into knowledge-work applications first.
STAGE 03 · FORECAST
Forecasting growth and its allocation across industries
We extend forward in two parts. First, a Logistic (S-curve) diffusion model for total global private AI investment: a standard three-parameter growth function (originally Verhulst, 1838; Rogers, 1962) that assumes annual flow grows at a rate proportional to the remaining room to grow. It produces an S-shape — slow start, rapid middle, deceleration toward a ceiling K. Fitted on global private investment, 2019–2025 (n=7, R² = 0.949). Second, an allocation model that distributes the forecast across the 15 industries using each sector’s Bass-diffusion velocity — the current rate of change of adoption from the Bass (1969) model, computed as (p + q·A)·(1 − A) where A is the current adoption share, p captures external influence (advertising, spillovers), and q captures imitation (peer effects). Higher velocity = more adoption still to happen per period.

Forecast — model properties The fitted curve describes the total annual flow of global private AI investment, not a cumulative sum. K is not estimated from the data; it is calibrated to a range of scenarios anchored at 2025 private investment ($344.7B, Stanford AI Index 2026) scaled by 3×, 5×, and 10×. Each scenario corresponds to a distinct economic story:

— Conservative (K = $1.03T): GenAI investment peaks near current levels and cools. The 2024–2025 surge is the sector's peak acceleration; the inflection has effectively passed (t₀ ≈ 2026.6), and annual flow decelerates toward $857B by 2030. This is the “GenAI investment bubble deflates” scenario.

— Central (K = $1.72T): Trajectory continues, maturity arrives late decade. Peak acceleration is still ~2.5 years ahead (t₀ ≈ 2028.3); annual flow reaches $1,157B by 2030 (80% CI $1,009B–$1,280B), with maturity and deceleration emerging in the early 2030s.

— Aggressive (K = $3.45T): AGI-transformation scenario, peak growth still ahead. Inflection doesn't arrive until 2030 itself (t₀ ≈ 2030.5); annual flow crosses $1.5T by 2030 and continues accelerating. Requires private AI investment to ultimately exceed Gartner's 2026 total-AI-spending forecast.

Fit quality (R²) ranges only from 0.947 to 0.953 across the three scenarios — seven observations cannot meaningfully distinguish between them. The scenario toggle lets you inspect how ceiling assumptions propagate to projections; the fit statistics above update with your selection.
Allocation forecast: where the projected flow is distributed across industries
Each sector’s share = (Bass velocity × sector share of global output) / total. Bass velocity = current rate of adoption growth. Sector share of global output approximates the addressable revenue pool. Shares sum to 100% of total projected 2026–2030 flow.
Manufacturing 16 percent, Financial Services 12 percent, Retail 10 percent lead the allocation.
Allocation — where it’s heading Manufacturing (16%) leads the allocation ranking, followed by Financial Services (12%) and Retail & Consumer (10%). Manufacturing ranks first not because of fast adoption growth (it’s mid-pack at 65%) but because it is the largest sector by share of global output (~15%) — a moderate adoption rate applied to a very large revenue pool produces a large absolute investment opportunity. Financial Services combines high adoption momentum (80%) with substantial output weight (14%). Retail & Consumer ranks third on the strength of its 10% output share and meaningful remaining adoption runway (68% in 2025).

A counter-intuitive result: Technology / Software ranks only 10th at 5% despite having the fastest adoption (92% in 2025) and the second-largest output share (12%). This is a feature of the Bass model: when adoption $A$ is near saturation, the velocity term $(1-A)$ collapses, so sectors with little remaining diffusion headroom receive small projected allocation shares. In other words, Tech has already absorbed most of its addressable AI investment; the next wave of growth goes to sectors with more room to adopt. At the other end, sectors with strong velocity but small output shares — Legal Services (~2%) and Environment / Climate (~2%) — receive small allocations because the revenue pool they represent is limited.

Note: this allocation is scenario-independent. The percentage shares above depend only on each sector’s adoption level, Bass parameters, and share of global output — none of which involve the saturation ceiling K. Switching between the Conservative, Central, and Aggressive scenarios above changes the total projected flow being allocated, but not the share each industry receives. The composition is the same regardless of whether the cumulative 2026–2030 flow turns out to be $3.3T, $4.0T, or $4.8T.
STAGE 04 · LABOR CONSEQUENCES
Employment shifts associated with AI investment and adoption
Finally, we turn to labor outcomes. BLS 2024–2034 employment projections (released August 2025) incorporate explicit AI-impact adjustments at the sector level. We pair 2025 sector AI adoption rates with projected 10-year employment growth to examine whether AI-leading sectors are projected to gain or lose employment. This is a descriptive pairing; we do not claim a causal effect of adoption on employment.

Labor consequences — key findings The scatter shows a mildly positive but noisy association between sector adoption and projected employment growth: high-adoption sectors (Tech, Professional Services, Healthcare) are projected to gain jobs (BLS MLR 2026). Aggregate BLS projections do not show AI leading to net sector-level losses in high-adoption sectors. The pattern is consistent with the complementarity literature (Acemoglu & Restrepo 2018, 2022; Autor 2024) — AI often complements rather than substitutes for labor in knowledge-intensive sectors, raising productivity and creating new task demand. Retail is the exception: high-velocity adoption paired with projected net job losses (−1.2%), consistent with the substitution literature (Frey & Osborne 2017; Acemoglu & Restrepo 2020) where AI directly replaces routine customer-facing labor.

Critical caveat — occupational churn hidden in sector totals. Sector aggregates mask occupational composition — that is, they show net jobs added or lost but not the mix of jobs being created and destroyed within a sector (occupational churn). Brynjolfsson, Chandar & Chen (2025) find early-career workers (ages 22–25) in AI-exposed roles — software developers, customer-service representatives, accountants — are already experiencing ~13% employment declines even while their sectors grow overall. This is exactly the complement-versus-substitute split at the occupational level: complements for senior experienced workers, substitutes for entry-level routine work. Sector-level growth numbers hide this composition shift entirely.

Synthesis — connecting the four stages

Stage 01 → 02: National diffusion capacity is extraordinarily concentrated — the US expanded AI investment intensity 11.3× since 2019 (0.11% → 1.24% of GDP) while most of the panel flatlined below 0.20% (Stanford AI Index 2025 & 2026; IMF real GDP). That capacity concentration translates directly into where global flows go: 83% of 2025 private AI investment landed in the US ($285.9B of $344.7B).

Stage 02 → 03: Within the global flow, capital concentrates in information-intensive industries (Technology, Financial Services, Professional Services) and knowledge-work functions (IT & Software Engineering, Marketing, Product). The logistic model is presented as three scenarios — conservative, central, and aggressive — anchored at 2025 private investment ($344.7B) scaled by 3×, 5×, and 10×, giving saturation ceilings of $1.03T, $1.72T, and $3.45T. Under the central scenario, inflection occurs in 2028.3 and 2030 annual flow reaches $1.16T. The Bass-velocity allocation model distributes projected flow toward sectors combining remaining adoption runway with large global output share — Manufacturing, Financial Services, and Retail & Consumer lead the 2026–2030 allocation.

Stage 03 → 04: The sector-level labor evidence is not a story of aggregate displacement — BLS projects the highest-adoption sectors to gain jobs through 2034 (BLS MLR 2026). But sector totals hide occupational composition: Brynjolfsson, Chandar & Chen (2025) document a 13% employment decline for AI-exposed workers aged 22–25 even in sectors that are growing. This is the complementarity-vs-substitution split (Acemoglu & Restrepo 2018, 2022; Autor 2024; Frey & Osborne 2017) playing out at the occupational rather than sector level.

Research-question answer: Global AI investment shapes diffusion patterns through three mechanisms — (1) concentrating national capacity in a small set of economies, (2) channelling capital into information-intensive sectors where adoption is easiest, and (3) restructuring occupational demand within otherwise growing sectors. The labor consequences are distributional, not aggregate, which means standard sector-level employment indicators are inadequate for monitoring them.
