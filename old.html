Research question

How does global AI investment shape the rate and pattern of AI adoption across industries and business functions — and what does that mean for labor?

We examine how global AI investment shapes the rate and pattern of AI adoption across industries and business functions. We build the argument in four stages: first a measure of national capacity; then concentration of global flows; then a forecast of both aggregate growth and its allocation; finally the labor consequences that follow from that trajectory.

STAGE 01 · CAPACITY
AI investment intensity as a proxy for national diffusion capacity
We construct AI investment intensity as private AI investment by country scaled by real GDP. Scaling by GDP lets us compare economies of very different sizes on the same footing — it’s a proxy for how much of a country’s productive capacity is being committed to AI, and therefore for the institutional ability to absorb and diffuse the technology.
Panel: 10 sovereign countries, 2019–2025. EU aggregate excluded — a 27-country aggregate cannot be meaningfully compared to single economies on a scaled axis.

Capacity — key findings The US expanded intensity 8.6× (0.10% → 0.88% of GDP, 2019–2025), concentrating global diffusion capacity. Singapore sustains ~0.27–0.37% of GDP as APAC’s AI hub despite being a small economy. China’s 2025 intensity (0.06%) is lower than its 2021 peak (0.09%) — export controls and macro pressure are compressing capacity. Germany and France are stuck below 0.08% — the diffusion-capacity gap that EU AI policy references is visible in the data.

Concentration — key findings Investment growth concentrates where information-intensity is highest: Tech (70%→92%), Professional Services (50%→82%), and Financial Services (55%→80%). Manual-heavy sectors (Agriculture 15%→42%, Real Estate 18%→46%) grow but from low bases. On functions, IT & Software Engineering jumped from 17% to 43% in four years — the clearest signal that capital is flowing into productivity-augmenting applications in knowledge work.
STAGE 03 · FORECAST
Forecasting growth and its allocation across industries
Having established where investment is now concentrated, we extend forward. Logistic (S-curve) diffusion model fitted on global private investment, 2019–2025 (n=7, R²=0.949). Allocation forecast combines each sector’s Bass-diffusion velocity with its GDP weight — capital chases sectors with the highest remaining adoption upside scaled by economic size.

Forecast — model properties Inflection at t₀ ≈ 2027 means peak annual acceleration is within 12 months; growth then decelerates toward the $1.2T ceiling as diminishing returns bite. Central 2030 estimate is $943B (80% CI $492B–$990B). The ceiling is externally anchored to Gartner’s $2.5T total-AI-spending forecast — private investment as ~20–30% of that total.

STAGE 04 · LABOR CONSEQUENCES
Employment and unemployment shifts associated with AI investment and adoption
Finally, we turn to labor outcomes. BLS 2024–2034 employment projections (released August 2025) incorporate explicit AI-impact adjustments. We pair sector adoption rates with their projected 10-year employment growth to examine whether AI-leading sectors are gaining or losing labor demand. We also flag the Brynjolfsson et al. early-career displacement finding — a 13% employment decline for ages 22–25 in AI-exposed occupations, a pattern invisible in aggregate sector numbers.

Labor consequences — key findings The scatter shows a mildly positive but noisy association between sector adoption and projected employment growth: high-adoption sectors (Tech, Professional Services, Healthcare) are all projected to gain jobs. Aggregate BLS projections do not show AI leading to net sector-level losses in high-adoption sectors — instead, the pattern is augmentation with occupational churn. Retail is the exception — high-velocity adoption paired with projected net job losses, consistent with automation substituting for labor in customer-facing roles. Critical caveat: sector aggregates mask the occupational distribution. Brynjolfsson et al. find early-career workers in AI-exposed roles (software developers, customer service, accountants aged 22–25) are already seeing 13% employment declines even while their sectors grow — a compositional effect invisible in sector totals.

Synthesis — connecting the four stages

Stage 01 → 02: National capacity (investment intensity) is extraordinarily concentrated — the US alone runs 8.6× the 2019 intensity, while most of the world flatlines. That capacity concentration translates directly into where the global flows go: 83% of 2025 private investment landed in the US.

Stage 02 → 03: Within the global flow, capital concentrates in information-intensive sectors (Tech, Finance, Professional Services) and knowledge-work functions (IT, Marketing, Product). The logistic forecast implies peak acceleration in 2027, with the next $600B allocated to sectors that combine high adoption velocity with substantial GDP weight.

Stage 03 → 04: The sector-level labor evidence is not a story of aggregate displacement — BLS projects the highest-adoption sectors to gain jobs through 2034. But sector totals hide occupational composition: early-career employment in AI-exposed roles is already declining 13%, and retail (high-velocity adoption, projected net job losses) signals where substitution effects can dominate.

Research-question answer: Global AI investment shapes diffusion patterns primarily through three mechanisms — (1) concentrating national capacity in a small set of economies, (2) channelling capital into information-intensive sectors where marginal returns are highest, and (3) restructuring occupational demand within otherwise growing sectors. The labor consequences are distributional, not aggregate, which makes standard sector-level indicators inadequate for monitoring them.

Data sources: Stanford AI Index 2025 & 2026 (investment); Mendeley datasets (adoption); U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics 2024–34 projections (labor); Brynjolfsson, Chandar & Chen 2025 (early-career displacement). Limitations: 10-country panel; adoption from self-reported surveys; country-level adoption in stage 1 is not directly measured; labor data is US-only; forecast uncertainty widens beyond 2027.
